# Fimbulwinter and Tin Production

Status: Draft
Spoiler Level: Full Canon

This page defines how Fimbulwinter affects tin production in HUMANE and how that pressure should be used narratively.

## Core Canon Rule

Fimbulwinter does not magically erase tin.

It damages the whole tin system: extraction, labor, charcoal, storage, transport, political trust, and bronze production.

Pure tin ingots may also suffer a cold-driven structural failure known in modern terms as **tin pest**, but bronze objects already alloyed with copper are mostly protected from this specific problem.

> The terror is not that tin vanishes. The terror is that tin can often be saved only by spending heat, labor, and order that starving people also need to survive.

## Scientific Anchor

Some substances can exist in more than one solid structure.

For compounds, this is often called **polymorphism**. For elements, it is more precisely called **allotropy**.

Tin is narratively useful because it has different solid forms:

| Form | Modern Description | Narrative Behavior |
|---|---|---|
| White tin | Metallic tin | Bright, useful, malleable, valuable for bronze making. |
| Gray tin | Non-metallic allotrope | Dull, brittle, powdery, diseased-looking, associated with cold damage. |

The shift from white tin to gray tin becomes favorable below about **13.2 °C**, though it is not instant and should not happen to every stored ingot. It usually needs sustained cold, time, and often seeding from existing gray tin.

This makes it ideal for HUMANE because the phenomenon can be frightening without becoming unrealistic.

## What Fimbulwinter Actually Breaks

Tin production is not one action. It is a chain.

Fimbulwinter can break that chain at several points at once.

### Mining and Streamworking

Cold, rain, frost, and mud make tin extraction more dangerous.

Likely effects:

- flooded workings,
- frozen or unstable ground,
- collapsed shallow pits,
- shorter working days,
- injured or sick workers,
- fewer people available for mining because food survival becomes urgent.

Cassiterite ore itself remains usable. The problem is access, labor, and safety.

### Fuel and Smelting

Smelting requires heat. Heat requires wood and charcoal.

During Fimbulwinter, fuel becomes a moral battlefield:

- charcoal can smelt ore,
- firewood can keep families alive,
- kilns and hearths compete with human bodies,
- smiths become politically important,
- stored fuel becomes a target for theft or tribute.

This creates one of the strongest HUMANE dilemmas:

> Do they burn the wood to save the metal, or save the children?

### Labor and Food

Hungry communities cannot maintain normal production.

Likely effects:

- miners abandon work to protect families,
- guards leave trade routes exposed,
- apprentices are pulled from training,
- smiths are guarded or controlled by chiefs,
- old bronze is repaired, hoarded, stolen, or melted down.

The tin shortage is therefore also a food shortage, a labor shortage, and a trust shortage.

### Storage: Tin Pest / Gray Rot

Pure tin ingots stored in cold places may begin to change.

In-world signs may include:

- dull gray patches,
- swollen or blistered surfaces,
- cracking edges,
- brittle fragments,
- gray powder in storage baskets,
- ingots that sound wrong when tapped.

The Pritani and Kelta do not understand crystal structure. They might describe this as:

- winter rot,
- graybite,
- tin death,
- the cold eating the silver,
- metal sickness.

Scientifically, the tin is not gone. If collected and remelted, damaged tin may be recovered. The tragedy is that recovery requires fuel and organized labor during the exact period when both are scarce.

### Transport and Trade

Tin only matters politically if it can move.

Fimbulwinter may disrupt:

- river crossings,
- coastal exchange,
- pack routes,
- wagon movement,
- animal health,
- storage security,
- trust between Pritana and Kelta.

A single load of tin can become a strategic object worth protecting, stealing, or killing for.

## Political Implications

Tin is one of Pritana's greatest forms of leverage.

If tin production falls, Pritana loses bargaining strength at the same time everyone needs bronze more desperately.

This destabilizes:

- Senara's authority,
- tribute systems,
- Kelta-Pritani negotiation,
- Odrin's economic planning,
- smithing networks,
- military readiness,
- civilian trust in leadership.

The conflict should not be simplified into greed. Each faction can be logically terrified.

Pritana fears losing autonomy.

Kelta fears losing bronze production.

Smaller groups fear being crushed between larger powers.

## Military and Technological Implications

Reduced tin access makes bronze rarer.

Expected consequences:

- old bronze is recycled,
- broken tools are repaired instead of replaced,
- bronze weapons become elite or ceremonial,
- flint, stone, bone, antler, hardened wood, leather, traps, and terrain knowledge regain importance,
- smiths become protected assets,
- theft of scrap bronze becomes serious.

This should make the world feel like it is not becoming primitive, but being forced into harsher selectivity.

People remember bronze. They simply cannot always afford it.

## Character Handles

### Alessia Odrinlyr

Alessia can recognize that the tin is not cursed but patterned.

Possible observations:

- tin stored near hearths survives better,
- cold outer stores show worse gray rot,
- bronze objects do not suffer the same failure,
- powder from damaged ingots can still be gathered,
- remelting can restore usefulness if fuel is available.

This fits Hyper Fabric because she does not need modern chemistry. She only needs to see the components of the problem and reassemble them into a working rule.

A possible internal logic line for her:

> The tin is not dying. It is changing. And if it can change once, it can be forced to change back.

### Erlen Dhalmurr

Erlen's pressure should be moral, not technical.

He may understand that saving tin could save tools, weapons, and future harvests, but the fuel used to restore tin could also warm living people now.

His dilemma becomes:

> What part of civilization do you burn to keep the rest alive?

### Senara Dhalmurr

Senara's leadership is pressured by visible material failure.

If Pritana's tin stores visibly decay, her people may interpret it as a sign that the old order is failing. She must manage fear, production, trade pressure, and hunger at once.

### Odrin

Odrin may treat tin instability as proof that decentralized survival is too fragile.

He can use the shortage to justify consolidation, rationing, guarded production, forced agreements, or militarized logistics.

His logic can be cruel without being stupid.

## Reader Experience

The reader should feel that the metal itself has become part of the weather.

Tin should not be a background resource only. It should be a visible sign of collapse:

- a gray handprint on an ingot,
- smiths arguing over fuel,
- children told not to touch the powder,
- leaders hiding damaged stores,
- bronze blades repaired too many times,
- a trader murdered for scrap,
- a ceremonial object melted because survival has become more sacred than symbolism.

## Guardrails

Do not overstate the science.

- Tin pest should affect pure tin, not all bronze.
- It should not destroy all tin instantly.
- Ore remains usable.
- The biggest damage comes from systems collapse: hunger, fuel, labor, transport, and politics.
- The phenomenon should feel like myth to the characters but remain explainable to the author.

## Thematic Use

Bronze is strength born from mixture.

Copper alone is not bronze. Tin alone is not bronze. Cooperation is the alloy.

Fimbulwinter punishes isolation by making every material dependency visible.

This connects directly to HUMANE's central pressure:

> What makes a human humane when cruelty becomes logical?
